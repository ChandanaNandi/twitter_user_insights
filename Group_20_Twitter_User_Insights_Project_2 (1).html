1)Objective of the Proposed Research¶

The "Twitter User Gender Classification" dataset's goal is to acquire insight into the gender distribution of Twitter users by examining their profiles and tweets. This dataset provides a plethora of information about Twitter users, such as their self-identified gender, their level of confidence in that identification, their tweets, profile descriptions, and a variety of other online presence-related factors. The major purpose of this study is to accurately classify or forecast the gender of Twitter users using this data.¶

A digital marketing firm or a social media platform wanting to improve their targeting tactics could be one potential stakeholder for this project. Understanding the gender demographics of their customer base allows them to build more successful and personalized marketing campaigns, adverts, and content. This data could also help them detect patterns, interests, and behaviors linked with different genders, allowing them to boost user engagement and happiness.¶

Academic academics or social scientists interested in investigating online behavior and gender dynamics could be another potential stakeholder. This dataset allows you to examine patterns in the type and frequency of tweets, profile descriptions, and user interactions across genders. Such research could provide useful insights on society views and habits in the digital age.¶

In essence, the fundamental goal of this research is to identify Twitter users' gender and provide significant insights into gender-related trends and behaviors on social media. This information may be of significant interest and utility to a wide range of stakeholders, including marketers, social scientists, and anybody seeking a deeper knowledge of the online world and its relationship to gender dynamics.¶

#Dropping the columns that have high missing values

#Imputing the missing values of gender column with most common gender
Most_Common_Gender = Dataset['gender'].mode()[0]
Dataset['gender'].fillna(Most_Common_Gender, inplace = True)

#Imputing missing values of the column 'description' with "Not Available"
Dataset['description'].fillna("Not Availble", inplace =True)

#Imputing missing values of the column '_last_judgment_at' with most common date

Dataset['_last_judgment_at'].fillna(Most_Common_Date, inplace=True)

from scipy.cluster.hierarchy import linkage, fcluster, dendrogram
import seaborn as sns

#Assigning the missing values of numerical columns with mean value of the column
DATA = New_Clean_Dataset[['_trusted_judgments', 'gender:confidence', 'profile_yn:confidence',
                 'fav_number', 'retweet_count', 'tweet_count', 'length_of_text', 'engagement_total']]

DATA = DATA.fillna(DATA.mean())

#Specifying the linkage method as ward
linkage_Method = 'ward'

#Defining the maximum number of clusters
Max_Clusters = 10

#Defining Threshold for stoping the cluster merging
threshold = 5.0

#Initializing each cluster with each of the data point as seperate cluster
Initial_Clusters = [[i] for i in range(len(DATA))]

#Performing the hierarchical clustering
results = []
for Number_Clusters in range(2, Max_Clusters +1):
  linkage_matrix = linkage(DATA, method = linkage_Method)

CLUSTERS = fcluster(linkage_matrix, t = threshold, criterion='distance')

results.append(CLUSTERS)

import numpy as np
def kmeans(DATA, k , max_iters=100):
  np.random.seed(0)
  Cluster_Centroids = DATA.sample(n=k)

  for _ in range(max_iters):
    DATA['CLUSTERS'] = DATA.apply(lambda row: np.argmin(np.linalg.norm(row - Cluster_Centroids, axis=1)), axis=1)

    New_Cluster_Centroids = DATA.groupby('CLUSTERS').mean()

    if Cluster_Centroids.equals(New_Cluster_Centroids):
      break

      Cluster_Centroids = New_Cluster_Centroids.copy()

  return DATA['CLUSTERS']
